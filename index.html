Executive Summary

Semester Growth · Key Takeaways · Future Goals

This semester in MGMT 316 pushed me to treat my life like a manager treats an organization: with clear goals, intentional plans, and steady course-correction. I started with three S.M.A.R.T. goals around my career, social habits, and physical health. Professionally, I committed to pursuing an economics-related internship and using Chapman’s resources, LinkedIn, and networking to make that happen. Personally, I set the goal of cutting back on bars and fraternity parties to one planned night out per week. Physically, I aimed to gain five pounds of lean muscle through structured lifting and nutrition.

As the semester unfolded, those goals forced me to grow. I learned that sending online applications was not enough; real progress came from conversations with professors, classmates, alumni, and recruiters, so I built a weekly system for targeted applications and deliberate “human touchpoints.” I also refined my social goal by scheduling nights out in advance, setting curfews, and tracking my next-day energy and focus so I could see the impact of my choices. A broken hand mid-semester forced me to rethink my fitness plan and pivot from muscle gain to maintaining strength and health with lower-body training, machines, cardio, and better nutrition.

Looking ahead, I am carrying these lessons forward. I now know I can design simple systems that keep me consistent even when life gets messy. My next steps are to secure a strong internship, continue limiting social nights to protect my performance, and reset my post-injury strength goals once my cast is removed. This course showed me that effective management is really self-leadership: planning, organizing, leading, and controlling my own life so I can show up as a better student, athlete, teammate, and future business professional.

Team Feedback & Growth

How Others Experience My Leadership

Throughout this course I received feedback from classmates and team members on how I show up in group settings. Their comments highlighted several consistent strengths: reliability, preparation, and a calm presence under pressure. Peers appreciated that I follow through on commitments, contribute thoughtfully rather than talking just to talk, and help keep the group organized and on track with deadlines. This connects strongly to my management focus on planning and organizing— skills I also used to structure internship applications and track my goals.

Feedback also pointed out growth areas that I have started working on. Teammates encouraged me to speak up earlier in discussions, share my ideas with more confidence, and delegate instead of taking on too much myself. Combined with my own reflections on leadership during my injury and social-life changes, this pushed me to practice being more vocal, setting clearer expectations, and being honest about my limits. Going forward, I plan to actively ask for feedback during projects and to use it as a regular “control” step—just like in the four functions of management—to keep improving how I lead and collaborate.

S.M.A.R.T. Goal Progress

Career · Social Balance · Health
Professional Goal – Economics Internship
Specific Measurable Relationship-Driven

Original goal: Secure an internship in economics (with a focus on finance, policy, or business analysis) by applying to targeted roles during winter or summer interterm, using Chapman resources, networking, and online platforms.

Progress & insight: I discovered that volume alone does not create opportunities—relationships do. Early on I mostly clicked “apply,” but the biggest momentum came when I reached out to professors and alumni. This led me to redesign my plan into a weekly system: two tailored applications and three meaningful touchpoints each week, plus a running bank of short stories I can use in interviews. I am still in the process of landing the internship, but I’m ending the semester with stronger networking habits and a clear structure to keep executing.

Personal Goal – Social Balance
Boundaries Accountability Energy Management

Original goal: Limit going to bars and fraternity parties to one planned night out per week, tracking outings on my phone to stay accountable and protect my health, finances, and focus.

Progress & insight: Most weeks I hit the target, especially when I chose my night in advance. The hardest times were high-event weekends, when “one night” could easily become a whole weekend. In response, I tightened the rule: pick my night by Wednesday, set an end time, and add one “non-party” hangout such as coffee, the gym, or dinner with a friend. I now track my next-day energy, focus, and productivity, which gives me evidence that smarter choices translate into better performance.

Physical Goal – Strength & Injury Management
Adaptability Health First Consistency

Original goal: Gain five pounds of lean muscle by December using progressive overload in the gym, weekly weigh-ins, and a high-protein diet.

Progress & insight: A broken hand in early October forced me to pivot from muscle gain to injury-smart training. Instead of abandoning the goal, I reframed success as maintaining fitness: focusing on lower-body work, machines, cardio, and adequate protein while my hand healed. I did not hit the original weight target, but I prevented regression and protected my long-term health. This taught me that adapting goals to reality is a strength, not a failure, and that discipline includes knowing when to adjust expectations.
